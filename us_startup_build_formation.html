This guide covers the essential infrastructure every serious startup needs from day one.

The Formation Stack
What you need to set up, in order, before you can operate
WEEK 1
Choose entity + state → Incorporate Delaware C-Corp is the default. File Certificate of Incorporation with Delaware Division of Corporations. Takes 24-48h standard, 1h expedited. Cost: ~$89 state fee + registered agent.
WEEK 1–2
Get your EIN (Employer Identification Number) The IRS issues this — it's your company's tax ID, equivalent to an SSN for the business. Required to open a bank account. Free via IRS.gov (online if you have a US SSN, or via Form SS-4 by fax for foreign founders).
WEEK 2
Open a business bank account Mercury is the standard choice for startups in 2025. Requires: Certificate of Incorporation + EIN + ID of all founders. 100% online. Fully functional in 1-2 days after approval.
WEEK 2–3

WEEK 3–4
Register in your operating state (Foreign Qualification) If incorporated in Delaware but operating in California, New York, etc., you must "foreign qualify" in that state. This means registering with the Secretary of State and paying that state's fees and taxes.
MONTH 2
Set up operational stack Accounting (QuickBooks / Bench), payroll (Gusto), legal (Clerky or Orrick), cap table management (Carta or LTSE Equity), and your first contracts (advisor agreements, NDAs, employment).
// THE MOST COMMON MISTAKE

Many founders — especially those coming from outside the US — delay incorporation while building the product. This is a trap. Every day you operate without a legal entity, you accumulate personal liability. Every investor conversation you have before incorporating is wasted if you end up with the wrong structure. Incorporate first, build second. It takes less than a week and costs under $500.

Entity Structures
The 4 main options — and why one dominates for VC-backed startups
TL;DR — If you want to raise VC money: Delaware C-Corp, period.

VCs, accelerators (including YC), and institutional angels are structured to invest in C-Corps. They have legal, tax, and fiduciary reasons not to invest in LLCs or S-Corps. If you ever want to raise from institutional investors or go public, the Delaware C-Corp is the only viable path. Every other structure has a conversion cost you'll pay later.

VC-BACKED STANDARD · RECOMMENDED
Delaware C-Corp
Corporation · Most common startup entity
The default for any startup planning to raise institutional funding. Delaware's Court of Chancery has centuries of corporate case law, creating predictability for investors. Allows unlimited classes of shares (common, preferred), ISOs for employees, and is fully compatible with VC term sheets.
  • VCs, YC, angels — will invest. No exceptions or negotiations needed
  • Unlimited share classes: common for founders, preferred for investors
  • ISO stock options for employees (massive tax advantage for team)
  • Delaware Court of Chancery: predictable, founder-friendly case law
  • QSBS (Section 1202): up to $10M tax-free capital gains for early investors
  • Double taxation: corp pays tax, then shareholders pay tax on dividends
  • More expensive to maintain: annual report fees, registered agent, Delaware franchise tax
GOOD FOR SMALL BIZ · NOT FOR VC
LLC
Limited Liability Company · Flexible but VC-incompatible
Excellent for small businesses, real estate, and lifestyle companies. Extremely flexible structure with pass-through taxation (profits go directly to members). But venture investors can't easily hold LLC units due to their own fund structures and tax reasons.
  • Pass-through taxation: no double taxation, profits taxed at member level
  • Very flexible operating agreement — can customize economics freely
  • Less compliance burden than a C-Corp
  • Great for consulting, real estate, small teams, family businesses
  • VCs (especially funds) structurally cannot invest — UBTI tax issue
  • No ISO stock options — can't offer the same equity incentives to employees
  • Converting to C-Corp later is costly, complex, and triggers tax events
SMALL BIZ · MAX 100 SHAREHOLDERS
S-Corp
S Corporation · Pass-through with restrictions
A C-Corp that elects special tax treatment. Has pass-through taxation like an LLC, but is a corporation. Severely limited: max 100 shareholders, only one class of stock, no foreign shareholders. Completely incompatible with VC funding.
  • Pass-through taxation — avoids corporate double tax
  • Self-employment tax savings for owner-operators
  • Max 100 shareholders — kills any VC round immediately
  • Only one class of stock — cannot create preferred shares for investors
  • No foreign shareholders — blocks international co-founders or investors
  • Almost never used for startups planning to scale
MISSION-DRIVEN · IMPACT COMPANIES
Benefit Corporation (PBC)
Public Benefit Corporation · Purpose + profit
A C-Corp that legally commits to a public benefit purpose alongside profit. Directors are shielded from shareholder pressure to maximize profits at the expense of mission. Increasingly popular with impact-focused startups. Delaware allows this structure.
  • Legal protection for mission-driven decisions that sacrifice profit
  • Compatible with VC funding — still a C-Corp structure underneath
  • Strong for B2B sales to ESG-conscious enterprise clients
  • Some traditional VCs may push back on mission constraints
  • Annual benefit reporting requirements
  • Not necessary for most startups — rarely a meaningful differentiator early on

Critical Legal Documents You Need at Formation
1
Certificate of Incorporation

Filed with Delaware Division of Corporations. Establishes the company, authorized share count, and par value. Keep authorized shares at 10,000,000 common to avoid high franchise tax. Public document — anyone can see it.

2
Bylaws

Internal governance rules: how board meetings work, quorum requirements, voting procedures. Not public. Standard YC-style bylaws are fine for early stage — don't over-engineer this.

3
Founder Stock Purchase Agreement + IP Assignment

Issues shares to founders at a very low price. Critically, includes an IP Assignment clause transferring all pre-existing IP to the company. Without this, investors will ask: "does the company actually own its own tech?" File the 83(b) election within 30 days.

4
Vesting Agreement

Standard: 4-year vesting with 1-year cliff. Means co-founders earn their shares over time — if someone leaves early, they don't walk away with a large equity stake. Industry-standard and required by most VCs before closing a round.

5
83(b) Election — DO NOT MISS THIS

Filed with the IRS within 30 calendar days of stock issuance. Elects to pay income tax on the shares at their current (very low) value rather than at vesting (potentially much higher value). Missing this deadline = potentially massive tax bill when shares vest. No extensions. No exceptions.

6
Employee/Contractor PIIA Agreement

Proprietary Information and Inventions Agreement. Every employee and contractor must sign this. Ensures the company owns anything they build while working for you. Without PIIAs from all contributors, your IP ownership is legally questionable — a deal-killer in due diligence.

🚨 The 83(b) Election — The Single Most Time-Sensitive Action in Formation

You have exactly 30 calendar days from the date shares are issued to file Form 83(b) with the IRS. Send it via certified mail, keep the postal receipt, and get a signed copy back from the IRS. Many formation services (including Stripe Atlas) will help you prepare it, but YOU are responsible for mailing it in time. This is the most commonly missed and most expensive mistake in startup formation.

Banking Options
The startup banking landscape has changed dramatically — here's the current state in 2025-26
Post-SVB landscape (March 2023 collapse)

Silicon Valley Bank's collapse in March 2023 was a wake-up call: concentration risk is real. Most sophisticated startups now maintain accounts at 2+ banks, keep operating expenses at one and reserves at another, and stay under the $250K FDIC insurance limit per institution. The old "just use SVB" advice no longer applies. Mercury has emerged as the new default for early-stage startups.

Neobanks & Fintech-First (Recommended for Early Stage)
Mercury
NEOBANK · PARTNER BANKS: CHOICE + EVOLVE
The de-facto standard for startups in 2025. Excellent UI, fast onboarding, free for most startups. Supports both US and non-US founders. Has Treasury product for yield on idle cash.
  • 100% online onboarding — no branch visit
  • Free checking + savings, wire transfers
  • International founders accepted (most countries)
  • Mercury Treasury: 4-5% APY on idle cash (T-Bills)
  • Excellent API and integrations (QuickBooks, Stripe, etc.)
  • Virtual + physical debit cards, immediate
  • Not a bank itself — deposits at partner banks (FDIC via them)
  • Some high-risk sectors rejected (cannabis, crypto exchanges, adult)
Brex
NEOBANK + CORPORATE CARD · STARTUP-FOCUSED
Strong combination of banking + corporate card. Famous for giving credit to startups without personal guarantees. Brex Card has best-in-class rewards for software/travel. Better fit for companies with VC funding already raised.
  • Corporate card with no personal guarantee needed
  • High credit limits based on bank balance, not credit score
  • Great rewards: 7x on rideshare, 3x on travel, software
  • Strong expense management and ERP integrations
  • Dropped many small startups in 2022 — focus on funded companies
  • More complex onboarding than Mercury
Relay
NEOBANK · SMB-FOCUSED
Excellent for small teams and bootstrapped startups. Multiple sub-accounts for budget management, strong bookkeeper access features. Good alternative or complement to Mercury.
  • Up to 20 checking accounts + 50 savings accounts
  • Great bookkeeper/accountant access and permissions
  • Physical and virtual debit cards per account
  • Less startup-native than Mercury — more SMB-focused
  • Fewer integrations with startup tools
Traditional Banks (When You Need Them)
JPMorgan Chase
TRADITIONAL BANK · STARTUP BANKING
The traditional bank most used by startups for secondary accounts. Good for: receiving large wire transfers, international banking, when your enterprise clients require a "real" bank. Startup Banking team can be helpful post-Series A.
  • Most stable — FDIC insured, too big to fail
  • International presence — useful for cross-border operations
  • Enterprise clients and investors trust it
  • Slow onboarding — requires in-person visit in many cases
  • Fees on everything — not startup-friendly pricing
  • Bureaucratic — slow to adapt to startup needs
First Republic (gone) → competitors
TRADITIONAL · POST-2023 ALTERNATIVES
First Republic Bank (acquired by JPMorgan in 2023) was the other classic startup bank alongside SVB. Its demise left a gap filled by SVB's HSBC acquisition, Silicon Valley Bridge Bank, and traditional banks trying to win startup clients.
  • HSBC Innovation Banking fills some of the SVB gap
  • First Citizens Bank acquired SVB's loan portfolio — active in venture lending
  • None are as startup-native as SVB or First Republic were
Silicon Valley Bank (reborn)
ACQUIRED BY FIRST CITIZENS BANK · 2023
SVB still exists under First Citizens Bank ownership. Maintains its startup and VC focus, venture debt products, and international banking. Still a credible option for Series A+ companies needing venture debt or international operations.
  • Best venture debt / credit lines for funded startups
  • Deep VC network relationships intact
  • Trust damaged post-collapse — most startups avoid as primary bank
  • Not recommended as sole banking relationship
Recommended Banking Strategy
The 2-Bank Rule: Mercury + one traditional bank
  • Mercury (primary): Day-to-day operations, payroll, vendor payments, cards. Keep 3–6 months of runway here.
  • JPMorgan Chase or similar (secondary): Emergency reserve, large wires, enterprise client expectations. Keep 6–12 months of runway here.
  • Mercury Treasury: Excess cash above operating needs → T-Bills via Mercury for 4-5% yield.
  • FDIC limit: Keep under $250K per account per bank. Above that, you're uninsured. Use Mercury's sweep accounts or split across institutions.
Note for Foreign Founders (Non-US)

Opening a US bank account as a foreign founder is possible but requires planning. Mercury and Brex are the most international-founder-friendly. You'll need: Certificate of Incorporation, EIN (even without a US SSN), valid passport, and proof of company address. Stripe Atlas bundles the Mercury account opening into the formation flow — the most frictionless path for non-US founders. Some countries are restricted — check Mercury's acceptance list before starting.

Equity & Cap Table
How ownership is structured — the foundation of every investor conversation
What is a Cap Table?

The cap table (capitalization table) is the definitive record of who owns what percentage of the company, and what type of shares they hold. It starts simple (founders only) and grows complex with each funding round. Every investor will want to see it before writing a check. Get it right from day one — retrofitting a messy cap table is expensive and embarrassing.

Key Funding Instruments
MOST COMMON PRE-SEED / SEED
SAFE
Simple Agreement for Future Equity · Y Combinator standard
Created by YC in 2013 and now the dominant early-stage instrument. Not a loan — no interest, no maturity date. Converts to equity at the next priced round. Simple, fast, cheap to close (<$2K in legal fees). Two key parameters: valuation cap and discount.
  • No interest accrual — not debt, doesn't need repayment
  • Closes in days, not weeks — minimal legal friction
  • Valuation cap: investor converts at the lower of cap or round price
  • Discount: typically 10-20% discount to next round price
  • YC post-money SAFE (2018) is now the standard — dilution is cleaner
  • Dilution is deferred — founders don't know exact % until conversion
ALTERNATIVE / OLDER STANDARD
Convertible Note
Debt instrument that converts to equity
A loan that converts to equity at the next round. Has interest rate (typically 4-8% per year), maturity date (typically 18-24 months), and a conversion cap/discount. More complex than a SAFE. Was the standard before SAFEs — still used by some angels and in some geographies.
  • Some investors prefer the debt structure — more familiar
  • Accrued interest increases investor return slightly
  • Has a maturity date — if no priced round, must repay or renegotiate
  • Interest accrual = more dilution at conversion
  • More complex and expensive to document than SAFE
SEED / SERIES A+
Priced Round
Series Seed, Series A — actual equity at set valuation
A "proper" venture round with a term sheet, a set pre-money valuation, and the issuance of Preferred Stock to investors. Takes 6-12 weeks and $20-50K in legal fees. Brings board seats, protective provisions, and complex governance. Required for rounds above ~$2-3M from institutional VCs.
  • Clear valuation and cap table — everyone knows their %
  • Preferred stock: liquidation preferences, anti-dilution protection
  • Formal board structure — more governance and credibility
  • Expensive: $20-50K+ in legal fees
  • Slow: 6-12 weeks to close vs. days for a SAFE
  • Complex terms: pro-rata rights, information rights, board composition
EMPLOYEE INCENTIVES
Stock Options (ISO / NSO)
The standard employee equity incentive in the US
Options give employees the right to buy shares at a set price (strike price = 409A fair market value). ISOs (for employees) have favorable tax treatment. NSOs (for contractors, advisors) are more flexible. Both vest over 4 years typically.
  • ISOs: taxed at capital gains rate (not income) if held correctly
  • Attracts top talent who accept lower salary for upside
  • Requires a 409A valuation (~$1,500-$3,000) to set legal strike price
  • Exercise window: typically 90 days post-termination (can extend)
  • Complex AMT implications for ISOs — employees need tax advice
Cap Table Management Tools
INDUSTRY STANDARD
Carta
carta.com · Free for early stage
The dominant cap table management platform. Handles equity issuance, 409A valuations, waterfall analysis, employee option grants, and investor reporting. Required by most Series A VCs. Free for simple cap tables, paid for larger companies.
VC-BACKED ALTERNATIVE
LTSE Equity (Pulley)
pulley.com · Free for early stage
Growing competitor to Carta with better UX and lower pricing. Increasingly accepted by VCs. Good choice if you want to avoid Carta's data practices (they've been criticized for using cap table data to approach customers).
BOOTSTRAPPED / SIMPLE
Spreadsheet
Founders only · Pre-seed
A Google Sheet is fine when you have 2 founders and no investors. The moment you have any outside investors or issue options, move to Carta or Pulley. Don't manage a complex cap table in Excel — the errors are costly and embarrassing in due diligence.
